**Introduction
Chemical reactions can be split into two general categories.
<strong>Exothermic reactions</strong> release energy (heat) as they run.<br> If you touch an exothermic reaction, the reaction will feel warm or hot because heat flows FROM the reaction INTO your hand.
<strong>Endothermic reactions</strong> absorb heat from the surroundings as they run.<br> If you touch an endothermic reaction, the reaction will feel cool or cold because heat flows FROM your hand INTO the reaction.
Reactions can also be classified into smaller categories. We will consider 5 <em>reaction types</em> on the following pages.<br> <strong>Synthesis, Decomposition, Singe Replacement, Double Replacement, Combustion</strong>
Each <em>type of reaction</em> shows a specific set of characteristics.
**Synthesis
In a <strong><em>synthesis</em></strong> reaction, two or more substances react to form a <strong>SINGLE product</strong>.
For example, iron reacts with oxygen to form iron (III) oxide:<br> <strong>4Fe + 3O<sub>2</sub> -> 2Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></strong><br> Only one compound (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) appears on the product side.
In a synthesis process, the reactants may be elements, compounds, or a mix of elements and compounds.
Here's a synthesis reaction where three compounds combine:<br> <strong>CaO + 2CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O -> Ca(HCO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub></strong>
Synthesis reactions are also called <strong><em>combination</em></strong> reactions.
Play the video to see another example of a synthesis reaction!<br> <strong>2Mg<sub>(s)</sub> + O<sub>2(g)</sub> -> 2MgO<sub>(s)</sub></strong>
**Decomposition
In a <strong>decomposition</strong> reaction, a <strong>SINGLE reactant</strong> breaks down into two or more simpler substances.
The <em>simpler substances</em> may be elements OR compounds.
For example, when heated, glucose (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub>) breaks down into carbon and water vapor:<br> <strong>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub> -> 6C + 6H<sub>2</sub>O</strong>
Decomposition also makes your baked goods light and fluffy! When heated, baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) breaks down to produce sodium carbonate, water vapor, and carbon dioxide gas.<br> <strong>2NaHCO<sub>3(s)</sub> -> Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3(s)</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>(g)</sub> + CO<sub>2(g)</sub></strong>
The CO<sub>2(g)</sub> creates helpful bubbles inside your dough!
<u>Quick concept check</u>: Is the decomposition reaction shown in the video <strong>exothermic</strong> or <strong>endothermic</strong>?
**Single_replacement
In a <strong>single replacement</strong> reaction, atoms of one element replace atoms of another inside a compound.
Metals replace other metals. Nonmetals replace other nonmetals.
For example: <strong>2Al + 3CuCl<sub>2</sub> -> 2AlCl<sub>3</sub> + 3Cu</strong> <br>The Al atoms (metal) replace the Cu atoms (also a metal) in CuCl<sub>2</sub>.
In <strong>2KBr + Cl<sub>2</sub> -> 2KCl + Br<sub>2</sub></strong>, the (nonmetal) Cl atoms replace the (nonmetal) Br atoms in KBr.
When listed first in a compound, hydrogen (H) is treated like a metal.<br> <strong>Mg + 2HCl -> MgCl<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub></strong> &emsp; <strong>Ca + 2H<sub>2</sub>O -> Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub></strong>
<u>Pro Tip</u>: Single replacement reactions always show an <strong><em>element and a compound</em></strong> on BOTH sides of the equation.
Need help identifying metals vs. nonmetals? Pull out your periodic table and Google that question!
**Double_replacement
In a <strong>double replacement</strong> reaction, two ionic compounds exchange cations.
For example: <strong>CoCl<sub>2(aq)</sub> + 2NaOH<sub>(aq)</sub> -> Co(OH)<sub>2(s)</sub> + 2NaCl<sub>(aq)</sub></strong><br>In this reaction, the Co<sup>2+</sup> ions and Na<sup>+</sup> ions trade places to pair with different anions on the product side.
Need to review <em>cations vs. anions</em>? Pull out your periodic table and Google that question!
In a double replacement reaction, one product will be a <strong>precipitate</strong> OR water.
A <strong>precipitate</strong> is a solid that forms when two solutions (aq) are mixed. In the example above, Co(OH)<sub>2(s)</sub> is the precipitate.
Here's an example of a double replacement reaction that forms water: <br><strong>HCl<sub>(aq)</sub> + NaOH<sub>(aq)</sub> -> NaCl<sub>(aq)</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>(L)</sub></strong>
**Combustion
In a <strong>combustion</strong> reaction, a substance <strong>burns in oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>)</strong>.
Each product of a combustion reaction contains the substance's original elements plus oxygen.
For example: <strong>4NH<sub>3</sub> + 7O<sub>2</sub> -> 4NO<sub>2</sub> + 6H<sub>2</sub>O</strong><br>Oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>) appears as a reactant, and each product contains oxygen atoms in the formula.
When a compound contains carbon (C) and hydrogen (H), the combustion products will include CO<sub>2(g)</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>(g)</sub>.
For example:<br> <strong>CH<sub>4</sub> + 2O<sub>2</sub> -> CO<sub>2</sub> + 2H<sub>2</sub>O</strong><br> <strong>C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH + 3O<sub>2</sub> -> 2CO<sub>2</sub> + 3H<sub>2</sub>O</strong>
Let's repeat the characteristics of a combustion reaction:<br> <strong>- O<sub>2</sub> appears as a reactant.</strong><br> <strong>- Oxygen (O) appears in every product.</strong>

**Other_reaction_types
<strong>Dissociation reactions</strong> occur when an ionic compound dissolves in water and splits into positive ions (cations) and negative ions (anions).
<strong>Acid/base neutralization reactions</strong> are a specific case of double replacement reactions. The products include a <strong>salt</strong> and (usually) water.
<strong>Oxidation-reduction reactions</strong> (redox reactions) occur when one atom loses electrons and another atom gains those electrons.
Redox reactions often overlap with the synthesis, decomposition, single replacement, and combustion categories.
